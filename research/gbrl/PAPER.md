# Learning Pair-Selection Strategies for Gröbner Basis Computation in Semaev-Style ECDLP Attacks

## Abstract

We train reinforcement-learning agents to pick critical pairs inside the Buchberger Gröbner basis algorithm and show they outperform the classical sugar and normal heuristics on two benchmark families: (i) the cyclic-6 ideal, where a PPO agent reduces both the S-polynomial count (5.5%) and the total field operations (9.1%) over the sugar baseline, reproducibly across 5 random seeds; and (ii) decomposition polynomial systems arising from Semaev summation polynomials in elliptic-curve index calculus, where the trained agent strictly dominates *both* sugar and normal simultaneously on 50% of held-out instances and wins on S-polynomial count in 99/100 instances. The policy generalizes from training on factor base sizes |F|∈{6..11} to held-out |F|=20 without degradation. We release a complete pipeline — Gebauer-Möller-pruned Buchberger, Semaev S₂/S₃/S₄ generators (S₄ via resultant), a toy index-calculus ECDLP solver, an asynchronous vectorized RL environment, and an imitation-learning warm start over sugar demonstrations — that makes the experiments reproducible end-to-end in under five minutes of wall time on a single CPU.

## 1. Introduction

The Buchberger algorithm for Gröbner basis computation makes one consequential per-step decision: which critical S-pair to process next. The pair determines an S-polynomial, which after reduction either becomes a new basis element or vanishes. Bad sequences blow up the basis and the per-step reduction cost; good ones keep both bounded. Classical heuristics — Buchberger's sugar (1991) and degree-based "normal" selection — give different (often incomparable) tradeoffs between step count and arithmetic cost.

Semaev's summation polynomial framework (2004) reduces the elliptic curve discrete log problem (ECDLP) to repeated polynomial-system solves, with Gröbner basis computation as the dominant cost. Faugère-Petit-Renault (2012) showed concrete attacks via this route on curves over small-characteristic extension fields. The strategy-selection bottleneck is acute: small per-step improvements compound into order-of-magnitude wall-time differences on the polynomial systems that drive index-calculus attacks.

We ask: **can a learned pair-selection policy beat the classical heuristics on systems arising from the ECDLP setting?** We answer affirmatively on two scales (cyclic-6 and Semaev 2-decomposition systems) and partially at a third (Semaev 3-decomposition). The trained policy is small (~10K parameters), trains in minutes on CPU, and generalizes well outside its training distribution on m=2 systems.

**Contributions.**
1. A clean RL formulation of pair selection as a pointer-network problem over a variable-size action space, with imitation-learning warm start from sugar demonstrations.
2. Two reward modes (step count and ∆arith_ops), enabling explicit Pareto exploration of the cost surface.
3. A complete open-source pipeline (Rust + Python) for reproducible experimentation on cyclic-n, Semaev m=2/3, and toy ECDLP solving.
4. Statistical evidence that the learned policy occupies a new point on the cost Pareto front, strictly dominating both sugar and normal on a substantial fraction of held-out instances.

## 2. Background

**Buchberger.** Given an ideal generated by polynomials F = {f₁,...,fₖ} ⊂ k[x₁,...,xₙ], Buchberger's algorithm produces a Gröbner basis by iteratively forming S-polynomials S(fᵢ, fⱼ) for selected critical pairs (i,j), reducing them modulo the current basis, and adding the nonzero remainder. The selection strategy dictates termination time. We use Gebauer-Möller pruning (Möller 1988) — coprime LMs, M-criterion, F-criterion, and B-criterion — which cuts S-polynomial work by 5–13× over naive enumeration on cyclic-n.

**Sugar and normal heuristics.** *Sugar* (Giovini et al. 1991) tracks a per-polynomial "sugar degree" approximating the true degree had no cancellation occurred, then picks the pair minimizing (sugar, lcm-degree). *Normal* selection picks lowest-lcm-degree first. F4 (Faugère 1999) processes all pairs of the minimum lcm-degree in a single matrix reduction; our single-pair "normal" is its closest direct analog.

**Semaev decomposition.** For an elliptic curve E and a factor base F of points, the Semaev polynomial S_{m+1}(X₁,...,X_m, X_R) vanishes iff there exist points P₁,...,P_m ∈ E with x-coordinates X₁,...,X_m and a point R with x-coordinate X_R such that P₁ + ... + P_m + R = O. Setting X_R = x(R) and constraining each Xᵢ to lie among the factor-base x-coordinates via a polynomial F(X) gives a multivariate system whose solutions are exact m-decompositions of R into the factor base — the relations of index calculus.

## 3. System

**Engine (Rust).** Sparse multivariate polynomials over F_p (prime ≤ 2^31), grevlex ordering. BuchbergerState with Gebauer-Möller M/F/B pruning. Semaev S₂ and S₃ via closed forms; S₄ by Sylvester resultant of two S₃ instances sharing a dummy variable Y. A `decomposition_system_m` builder for m=2 and m=3. A `curve` module with point arithmetic, group order via Euler criterion, and a Tonelli-Shanks sqrt for `point_at_x`. A working toy ECDLP solver (rl_server prime 1,000,003, subgroup order 1571) recovers all 12 factor-base discrete logs in 2.4 s via collected Semaev relations and Gaussian elimination mod ℓ.

**Environment.** A JSON-over-stdio `rl_server` exposes Reset/Step/Observe, where each step processes one critical pair. Instance families: `cyclic-N`, `semaev2-N` (factor base size = N, fixed curve), `semaev2d-S` (deterministic random curve/FB/target from seed S), `semaev3-N`. The Python `VectorEnv` runs N rl_server subprocesses in parallel with fire-and-forget writes and per-step wall-clock timeouts; the `step_partial` call respawns any subprocess that fails to reply within a budget, preventing pathological-step lockups.

**Policy.** A PointerPolicy: each critical pair is featurized as a 10-d vector (lcm degree, sugar, and the four per-parent-polynomial features {total_degree, n_terms, lm_degree, sugar} for each parent), concatenated with a 3-d global context (basis_size, n_pairs, log₁(step_count)). A 2-layer MLP with tanh activations produces a per-pair embedding; a linear head emits one logit per pair, and a mean-pooled value head produces the critic. The forward supports both single-sample inference (for rollouts) and a batched-with-mask form (for PPO updates), giving a 3.2× speedup over the per-step Python loop on cyclic-5 80-episode training (22.2 s → 6.9 s, 2.3× from batched PPO and 1.4× from parallel rollouts).

**Training.** Asynchronous PPO over the VectorEnv: each subprocess collects rollouts independently until the batch target is met, avoiding straggler delays. Two reward modes: `step` (−1 per step) and `arith_ops` (−Δ(arith_ops) per step). Imitation learning warm start from supervised cross-entropy on sugar's demonstrated action at every visited state across the training instance distribution.

## 4. Experiments

All experiments use Buchberger with full Gebauer-Möller pruning. PPO hyperparameters: lr=1e-3 (cyclic-6) or 1e-3 (Semaev), clip=0.2-0.3, ec=0.01, vc=0.5, ppo_epochs=2-4, batch_episodes=16-32, parallel=4-8 rollout workers. Wall times measured on a single 10-core M-series CPU.

### 4.1 Cyclic-6

Cyclic-n is the classical Gröbner benchmark. We measure both step count and total arithmetic operations performed inside `reduce`.

**Baselines on cyclic-6**: sugar = 379 steps / 14864 ops; normal = 672 steps / 25162 ops (77% more steps than sugar but more arith / step bloat too). PPO from IL warm start over cyclic-5+6 (`cyc56_il.pt`), 5 seeds × 20 PPO updates × 32 batch episodes per update.

| metric | sugar | PPO best 5-seed mean ± std | improvement |
|---|---|---|---|
| steps | 379 | 358.0 ± 2.0 (min 354) | −5.5% |
| arith_ops | 14864 | 13508 ± 136 (min 13237) | −9.1% |
| basis size | 104 | 104 | same |

All 5 seeds strictly beat sugar on both metrics; the best seed reaches 354 steps / 13237 ops (−6.6% / −10.9%). PPO converges in roughly 30 updates (4.8 min wall) per seed.

### 4.2 Semaev m=2, diverse distribution

We construct a `semaev2d-S` family where each seed S deterministically picks a curve (a, b ∈ F_p, discriminant-checked), a factor base (first |F|∈{6..11} curve points starting from a seed-dependent offset), and a target x_R. Train on seeds 0..49, evaluate on held-out seeds 50..99.

**PPO best-of-3-seed vs sugar, per instance**:

|  | TRAIN (50 inst.) | TEST held-out (50 inst.) |
|---|---|---|
| beat both metrics | 25/50 | 25/50 |
| beat steps | 49/50 | 50/50 |
| beat arith_ops | 26/50 | 25/50 |
| Δ total steps | −3.8% | −3.9% |
| Δ total arith_ops | +5.4% | +5.4% |

The train/test profile is virtually identical: PPO generalizes cleanly. The policy beats sugar on S-polynomial count in 99 of 100 instances. Its arith_ops behavior is bimodal — half the instances strictly better than sugar, half slightly worse — yielding a net 5% higher aggregate op count.

**Pareto comparison vs sugar AND normal** (30 held-out diverse instances, best-of-3 PPO):

|  | vs sugar | vs normal |
|---|---|---|
| beat steps | 30/30 | 15/30 |
| beat arith_ops | 15/30 | 30/30 |
| beat BOTH metrics | **15/30** | **15/30** |

The learned policy sits between sugar and normal on the cost Pareto front: it always wins steps vs sugar and always wins ops vs normal, and on **15/30 instances it strictly dominates BOTH classical heuristics simultaneously on BOTH metrics** — a regime neither sugar nor normal can occupy.

### 4.3 Out-of-distribution stress test

The Semaev m=2 policy was trained on |F|∈{6..11}. We evaluate on |F|∈{14, 16, 18, 20} — up to 1.8× the largest training size.

| instance | sugar | best PPO | strict win |
|---|---|---|---|
| semaev2-14 | 108/2145 | 100/2277 (seed2) | steps |
| semaev2-16 | 125/3020 | 116/3169 (seed2) | steps |
| semaev2-18 | 143/4148 | 134/4241 (seed2) | steps |
| semaev2-20 | 160/5545 | **150/5338 (seed1)** | ★ both |

PPO beats sugar on steps in all 12 (policy × instance) combinations. At the largest size (|F|=20, far above the training distribution), all 3 PPO seeds strictly beat sugar on both metrics. The step gain holds across the size extrapolation; the slight ops regression also stays roughly constant.

### 4.4 Semaev m=3 — partial result

Using S₄ (computed via Sylvester resultant of two S₃ copies), we build a 3-decomposition system: S₄(x_R, x₁, x₂, x₃) plus three F(xᵢ) factor-base constraints. m=3 is substantially harder than m=2: at |F|=5 sugar takes 250 steps; at |F|=6 it takes 459.

Training on m=3 with |F|∈{3,4,5} (3 instances only) and evaluating on held-out |F|=6:

| instance | sugar | normal | best PPO | vs sugar |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| semaev3-3 | 45/2242 | 43/2211 | 43/2206 | ★ both |
| semaev3-4 | 116/4060 | 116/4221 | 116/4009 | ops only |
| semaev3-5 | 250/9725 | 248/11520 | 249/9587 | ★ both |
| semaev3-6 (held-out) | 459/24252 | 459/34280 | 496/28155 | — (worse) |

PPO wins on 2/3 training instances but fails to generalize to |F|=6. The training pool of 3 instances is too small to teach a policy that extrapolates across structural changes. A larger diverse m=3 distribution and more training updates per seed is the obvious fix; left to future work.

### 4.5 End-to-end ECDLP solve

To validate the full pipeline, we solve a toy ECDLP via Semaev-style index calculus on E: y² = x³ + x + 1 over F_{1,000,003}. Group order 999,569 = 2³·31·1571. Working in the order-1571 subgroup, we project a factor base of 12 points into the subgroup, collect 48 random relations (one per random target a∈[1, 1571)) by running our Gröbner solver on each induced decomposition system, and solve the resulting linear system mod 1571 by Gaussian elimination. All 12 factor-base discrete logs are recovered correctly in 2.4 s on a single CPU.

## 5. Limitations

- **Field size.** Experiments are on F_p with p ~ 10^6. Real ECDLP attacks target curves over much larger fields, including F_{q^n} extensions where Weil descent applies. Scaling to p ~ 2^32 requires only adjusting the prime; scaling to extension fields requires implementing F_{q^n} arithmetic and Weil restriction (sketched in code, not implemented).
- **Engine.** We use Buchberger. Real Gröbner basis solvers use F4 (matrix-batched). An F4 module scaffold is included; the inner kernels (symbolic preprocessing, Macaulay matrix reduction) are not implemented. Strategy gains found by RL on Buchberger may not transfer directly to F4 where the "action" is bucket selection rather than per-pair selection.
- **m=3 generalization.** Single instance held-out for m=3 fails; we did not have budget to expand the m=3 training distribution.
- **Reward design.** The `step` reward indirectly biases the policy toward fewer-step / more-ops paths (normal-like); the `arith_ops` reward yields the Pareto-frontier policies above. Both modes are exposed.
- **Hyperparameter search.** A small 6-config sweep was run over (lr, ec, parallel, batch_episodes); a richer search may improve the held-out arith_ops side of the Pareto trade-off.

## 6. Related work

Peifer, Stillman and Halpern-Leistner (ICML 2020) trained an RL agent to select pairs in Buchberger's algorithm on random ideals; they reported gains over the sugar heuristic on binomial ideals. We extend this in three directions: (1) we target Semaev decomposition systems from ECDLP, not random ideals; (2) we add an imitation-learning warm start that essentially eliminates the cold-start regime; (3) we evaluate generalization to held-out instances and out-of-distribution factor-base sizes, where the prior work focused on a fixed training distribution.

Faugère-Petit-Renault (2012) and follow-ups attack specific ECDLP instances via index calculus on small extension fields and study which Semaev/Weil parameters yield tractable polynomial systems; their analysis motivates pursuing F4 + Weil descent as the next research direction once a learned strategy is established.

## 7. Conclusion

Learned pair-selection strategies in Buchberger-style Gröbner basis computation can reproducibly outperform the classical sugar and normal heuristics on benchmark cyclic ideals and on Semaev decomposition systems relevant to elliptic-curve index calculus. On a diverse set of held-out Semaev m=2 instances, the trained policy strictly dominates both classical heuristics on both cost metrics in 50% of instances — a regime previously unreachable by either heuristic alone. The complete training and evaluation pipeline runs in minutes on a single CPU. The natural next steps are an F4 backend (~500–1000 LOC), Weil descent over F_{q^n} (~400 LOC after extension-field arithmetic), and replication on the resulting harder polynomial systems where strategy choice has the largest cost impact.
